## 🔐 The VAULTED Protection Guide

A plain-language reference updated as new scam types emerge. No jargon. No filler. Just what you actually need to know.

### Crypto ATM Scams
These machines look legitimate — they're in gas stations, pharmacies, and strip malls. They're not regulated the way banks are, and scammers know it.

- **No legitimate person will ever tell you to deposit cash into a crypto ATM.** Not a government agency, not a utility company, not a bank, not tech support. Ever.
- Once cash goes into one of these machines, it is gone. There is no dispute process, no chargeback, no reversal.
- If someone calls you and says you owe money that can only be paid via a crypto kiosk, hang up immediately. Then report it to the FTC at reportfraud.ftc.gov.

### Phishing & Smishing
Phishing is a fraudulent email designed to steal your login or payment information. Smishing is the same thing via text message. Both are built to look real.

- **If a link came from a text message, don't click it.** Go directly to the company's official website by typing the address yourself.
- Real banks, government agencies, and utilities do not ask you to verify your account by clicking a link in a text.
- Check the sender's email domain character by character — one letter off is intentional. "paypa1.com" is not PayPal.
- When in doubt, call the company directly using the number on their official website — not the number in the message.

### Romance Scams
These scams can go on for months or years. The scammer builds a real emotional relationship — then cashes it in.

- **Anyone who builds a relationship online and eventually asks you to send money is running a scam.** This holds true even if you've been talking for months and even if they've sent you gifts first (those gifts are often paid for with stolen cards).
- Common scripts: "I'm stuck overseas on a job and need you to wire funds." "I'm in the military and can't access my account." "There's a customs fee to release the package I sent you."
- Do a reverse image search on every photo of someone you haven't met in person. Most romance scam photos are stolen from real social media accounts.
- If you've already sent money, contact your bank immediately, then report to the FTC.

### Impersonation Fraud
Scammers impersonate the IRS, Social Security Administration, Medicare, your bank, Amazon, Microsoft, and local law enforcement — often convincingly.

- **Government agencies do not call demanding immediate payment.** The IRS contacts you by mail first. SSA will never threaten to suspend your Social Security number over the phone.
- If someone claims to be from your bank, hang up and call the number printed on the back of your card. Not the number they gave you. Your card.
- **Urgency is the weapon.** "You'll be arrested in two hours." "Your account will be closed today." "Act now or lose everything." Scammers manufacture panic specifically to stop you from thinking clearly and calling someone you trust.
- Legitimate companies and agencies will never ask for payment via gift cards, wire transfer, or cryptocurrency.

### Account Takeover & Credential Theft
Your usernames and passwords are more valuable than you might think — and there are entire operations built around stealing them.

- Use a different password for every account. A password manager makes this easy.
- Enable two-factor authentication (2FA) on every account that offers it — especially email, banking, and social media.
- If you get a 2FA code you didn't request, someone is actively trying to get into your account. Don't share the code with anyone, and change your password immediately.
- Check haveibeenpwned.com to see if your email has appeared in known data breaches.

## 🚨 Report a Scam

If you've been targeted or know someone who has:

- **FTC (Federal Trade Commission):** [reportfraud.ftc.gov](https://reportfraud.ftc.gov)
- **FBI Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3):** [ic3.gov](https://www.ic3.gov)
- **Consumer Financial Protection Bureau:** [consumerfinance.gov/complaint](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/complaint/)
- **Identity Theft:** [identitytheft.gov](https://www.identitytheft.gov)

Reporting matters. Every report helps investigators identify patterns and take action.
